# AI Adoption Last Mile

Status: active
Last Updated: 2026-05-17

## Rough Hypothesis

AI tools fail because companies do not know how to make them part of real work.

## Refined Hypothesis

Organizations buying or piloting AI tools struggle to convert demos into durable workflow replacement because internal teams lack the time, process knowledge, and implementation support to redesign work around AI; they need forward-deployed adoption help and reusable implementation playbooks, not just better models.

## Background Change

X bookmarks include discussion of forward-deployed AI engineers, Copilot meeting assistance, Codex service workflows, and the idea that one person may soon cover more of a software team's work. These point to adoption and workflow redesign as the constraint.

## Target User

- AI transformation leads
- Developer productivity teams
- Vendors selling AI workflow automation
- Teams trying to move from AI PoC to production operations

## User / Market Estimate

Primary User:
- AI adoption lead, vendor success owner, or internal champion responsible for turning a PoC into daily work.

Buyer:
- Business department, transformation office, vendor, or executive sponsor.

Adoption Unit:
- organization

Estimated User Count:
- large because AI PoC-to-adoption failure appears across many functions and industries.

Business Scale:
- broad to enterprise because failed adoption can waste large budgets, but the current hypothesis is too broad for a focused product wedge.

Spike Type:
- mixed: user count and business scale are large, but Hachiware fit is weaker unless narrowed to developer-agent adoption.

## Trigger Moment

An AI pilot works in a demo or isolated task, but fails to replace a recurring business workflow because ownership, training, data access, process redesign, and success metrics are unclear.

## Concrete Pain

- PoCs end without operational adoption.
- Internal IT teams can configure tools but not redesign the work.
- Business teams cannot translate AI capabilities into repeatable processes.
- Vendors are blamed for poor outcomes even when the product works technically.

## Current Workarounds

- Generic training sessions
- Vendor success calls
- Internal champions
- One-off prompt libraries
- Consulting-heavy implementations

## Why Workarounds Fail

- Training does not embed AI into actual daily work.
- Champions have limited bandwidth and authority.
- Prompt libraries do not solve process ownership or measurement.
- Consulting does not always leave reusable assets behind.

## Counter-Evidence

- This may be a services business rather than a scalable product opportunity.
- Large vendors may bundle adoption support into enterprise contracts.
- Hachiware Labs may be more differentiated in developer-agent tooling than broad enterprise adoption.

## Falsification Conditions

- Interviews show AI PoCs fail mainly because model quality is insufficient, not because of adoption last mile.
- Customers do not want reusable playbooks or implementation artifacts.
- Forward-deployed work cannot be productized for Hachiware Labs.

## Hachiware Labs Fit

This fits Nagomi and refina if positioned as practical workflow adaptation. It can also connect to Argus by measuring before/after process evidence and AI-generated work quality.

## Validation Questions

- What causes AI PoCs to die after initial enthusiasm?
- What reusable artifact would help: process map, prompt pack, workflow KPI, training plan, or operational checklist?
- Who pays for adoption help: vendor, customer department, or IT?
- Can developer-agent adoption be the narrow wedge?

## MVP / Experiment Ideas

- Create a "PoC to Workflow" checklist for one AI coding or meeting workflow.
- Interview 5 people who ran AI PoCs and ask what blocked adoption.
- Package one forward-deployed implementation as reusable Markdown templates and metrics.

## Message

The hard part of AI adoption is not the demo; it is turning the demo into a daily workflow people actually use.
